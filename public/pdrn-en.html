PDRN: Hype or Real Skincare?

It started when Jennifer Aniston and the Kardashians were rumored to be fans of "salmon sperm facials." Now PDRN — often called "salmon DNA" — is one of the fastest-growing ingredients in skincare, with searches up over 1,400% between 2024 and 2025.[1] But does it work in a bottle, or is it mostly marketing? Here's the honest breakdown.

In short: PDRN has real evidence — but mostly as an injectable at a skin clinic. In a serum, the molecules are likely too large to penetrate deeply, so expect nice hydration and glow, not "injection results." Safe for most people, and a pleasant care step — just with realistic expectations.

What is PDRN, really?

PDRN stands for polydeoxyribonucleotide — small, purified fragments of DNA, most often extracted from salmon.[2] Before you wrinkle your nose: what goes on your face is a highly purified, processed version where proteins that could irritate the skin have been removed.[2] Because the DNA structure resembles our own, it's considered very skin-friendly.

The ingredient was originally used in regenerative medicine — to help wounds and tissue heal.[3] That's the background the skincare industry is now building on.

Does PDRN work — what does the research say?

Here it's important to separate two very different things:

✓ As an injectable: good evidence

Injectable PDRN (known under brand names like Rejuran) has the strongest evidence, including studies showing improvement in skin texture, elasticity and firmness through a well-described mechanism.[4] This is a clinic treatment — not something you do at home — and it isn't FDA-approved for cosmetic use in the US, though it's widely used in South Korea.[4]

⚠️ As a serum: much weaker basis

This is the part the marketing tends to skip. The skin's outermost layer follows a "500-Dalton rule": molecules larger than that generally don't cross intact skin. PDRN fragments are usually too large, so much of the improvement people experience from a PDRN serum likely comes from the hydrating and barrier-strengthening ingredients around it — not the PDRN itself.[4]

Newer research is looking at smaller PDRN fragments (e.g. from lactic-acid bacteria or plants) that may absorb better,[5] but this is early, and most cosmetic studies are small and short.

Honest summary: A PDRN serum is safe and can give nice hydration and glow over 2–4 weeks. But don't expect a jar to match a clinic injection — they're two different things. If you have a fish allergy, check with a doctor, or choose a vegan version (e.g. ginseng-based).[1]
